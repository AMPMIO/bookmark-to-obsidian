# Everything I've learned about LLM reasoning after 2 years of research

## Summary

Everything I've learned about LLM reasoning after 2 years of research [thread]

1/ Chain-of-thought prompting works because it forces the model to allocate more compute to hard steps — not because it "thinks" differently.

2/ The biggest misconception: people think longer chains = better reasoning. Not true. Concise, structured chains outperform rambling ones.

3/ Self-consistency (sampling multiple CoTs and taking the majority answer) is underused. It's a free accuracy boost on math and logic tasks.

4/ Models don't generalize reasoning to truly novel domains. They pattern-match to training data. This is both a feature and a limitation.

5/ The most reliable way to improve reasoning: give the model a worked example in the prompt. One good example > elaborate instructions.

6/ Future direction: process-reward models (PRMs) that score reasoning steps individually, not just final answers. This is where the field is heading.

Key takeaway: Reasoning in LLMs is a skill that can be elicited and shaped through prompting — but it's not the same as human reasoning.
